Online Workshop II — Day 1 Report

March 26, 2026  |  AI for Environmental Monitoring, Urban Systems & Sustainability Education

The first day of the SustAInability Online Workshop II brought together students, researchers, and practitioners from across Europe, Asia, and Africa for a morning of talks, live demonstrations, and interdisciplinary exchange. Six presentations covered a broad spectrum — from GIS-based agricultural analysis and AI-assisted land-use forecasting to augmented reality in education and AI tools for environmental learning.

Project Overview & Social Impact

The session opened with a presentation by Gergana, who provided a concise overview of the SustAInability project — summarising progress to date and highlighting the social dimension of the work. Her contribution set the tone for the day by framing the project not only as a research initiative, but as one with tangible benefits for real communities.

GIS and Sustainable Agriculture

Katharina Krome (SRH University Heidelberg) presented her work on using QGIS to support more sustainable agricultural decisions. By combining soil composition, temperature data, land use, and livestock information, she identified regions with strong potential for cultivating yellow sweet lupine — a nitrogen-fixing crop with environmental benefits. Areas such as Dorsten and Haltern am See emerged as particularly suitable. The presentation demonstrated how spatial data analysis can directly inform practical decisions in sustainable land management.

AI-Assisted Forecasting for Land Use

Prof. Fatih Sünbül presented a framework for AI-assisted land-use forecasting that integrates expert knowledge, machine learning models, and IoT sensor data. His talk addressed key pressures on land systems — including urbanization, tourism, and climate change — and argued that combining human expertise with AI yields more reliable and context-sensitive predictions than either approach alone. The session also featured a contribution from Dr. Khan (Malaysia), who added an international perspective to the discussion.

Augmented Reality & AI in Practice

Dr. Leila Mekacher presented applications of augmented reality and AI, including live demonstrations that gave participants a direct look at how these technologies function beyond theory. The demos illustrated how AR can make learning environments more interactive and engaging — a theme that resonated strongly with the workshop's focus on education and practical application.

AI for Environmental Education

The final presentation came from Dr. Issam Dridi (Tunisia), who examined the role of AI in environmental education. He outlined key challenges — the complexity of environmental systems and the gap between knowledge and action — and showed how AI tools such as simulations, chatbots, and interactive platforms can help bridge them. Concrete examples included water quality monitoring projects and renewable energy initiatives where students work directly with real-world data, making learning both more practical and more meaningful.

Key Takeaway

Across all six presentations, a shared message emerged: AI and data are increasingly essential for addressing sustainability challenges — but technology alone is not sufficient. Effective application requires strong education, interdisciplinary collaboration, and people who understand both the tools and the contexts in which they are used.
